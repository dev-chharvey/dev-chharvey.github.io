Listening Reports

These reports are my reactions to digital recordings of classical music. They are listed by composers' time period.

Bach, Johann Sebastian (1685–1750)

Invention No. 8 in F Major

index
BWV 779
recorded
Christopher Taylor, 2006
report submitted
Chris Harvey, December 17, 2008

Bach Inventions are short counterpoint pieces with two voices, and No. 8 is an excellent example. The subject, answer, and counter-subject interact in such a way that they fit together to sound like melody and harmony. Taylor makes the entrances of each subject plainly obvious to the listener so he/she can identify it at once. He swiftly moves through this upbeat invention, only pausing slightly at cadence points.

Prelude & Fugue No. 21 in B-Flat Major (from WTC Book I)

index
BWV 866
recorded
Angela Hewitt, 2007
report submitted
Chris Harvey, December 17, 2008

Hewitt does a great job emphasizing the outer voices in the homophonic sixteenth-note passages in the Prelude. Her dynamic contrast adds excitement to the piece, even when marked piano. She also has excellent contrast in articulation in the Fugue. Like Taylor, she makes the entrances of subject and answer obvious. Her performance is always moving in some direction, never stationary, even during strettos and episodes.

Prelude & Fugue No. 7 in E-Flat Major (from WTC Book II)

index
BWV 876
recorded
Angela Hewitt, 2007
report submitted
Chris Harvey, December 17, 2008

Although the Prelude is a quick-moving piece, Hewitt remains calm and composed, and keeps a consistent tempo. She adds variety to articulation patterns throughout the Prelude. She announces the return of the piece's theme in a blissful manner and with a change in articulation. Again, Hewitt makes this difficult four-voice Fugue seem easy to play. The Subject appears in all four voices multiple times, and can be easily identified. Her tempo is slightly faster than marked, but she pulls it off very well.

Haydn, Joseph (1732–1809)

Sonata No. 38 in F Major

index
H. XVI/23
composed
1773
recorded
Marc-André Hamelin, 2007
report submitted
Chris Harvey, December 17, 2008

Haydn wrote his music in a way that is open to interpretation. In this sonata, he leaves out almost all dynamics and articulation. Haydn has a talent for surprising the listener with subito dynamics and other musical formatting. Hamelin plays with a great contrast in articulation, ranging from spiky staccati to fluid legati. At times, he may hold some notes longer than expected. He seldom uses the pedal, which is accurate for the Classical time period. While there is no correct answer, Hamelin's dynamic markings differ from mine. He plays the third movement impressively fast.

Sonata No. 43 in E-flat Major

index
H. XVI/28
composed
1776
recorded
Lola Odiaga, 1995
report submitted
Chris Harvey, March 12, 2010

Odiaga performs this sonata on the fortepiano, an instrument suitable for the context in which the piece was written. For some reason, the fortepiano is tuned one semitone flat, so the piece sounds as if in D Major.

Her choice of articulation contradicts my own, but is acceptable for a Haydn sonata. Ornaments, trills, and turns are taken faster than normal, and in addition, placed before the beat rather than on the beat. Her choice of dynamics is conventional and expected by the listener.

The second movement is played in a hasty manner. Odiaga sees not to pay much attention to phrasing or articulation, and every turn is played too fast and before the beat, as grace notes. The melody in the Trio section is legato and slurred throughout. She ends every movement with a molto ritardando, which would be appropriate for maybe a Baroque-era piece, but I personally choose not to alter the tempo when closing the movements.

The third movement, marked Finale: Presto, is played carefully and almost tentatively. The performer did an excellent job with articulation and liveliness in this movement, although lacking in dynamic variation. The entire third movement builds speed progressively throughout the movement, which may or may not be intentional.

Beethoven, Ludwig van (1770–1827)

Sonata in d minor, “Tempest”

index
Op. 31 No. 2
recorded
Vladimir Ashkenazy, 2006
report submitted
Chris Harvey, December 17, 2008

Written near the end of Beethoven's first of three periods, this sonata leans more toward the Classical era. The forms of each movement in this sonata follow those of traditional classical sonata structure. Ashkenazy shows excellent dynamic contrast and does a good job bringing out the melody where needed. However, he uses much more pedal than necessary.

Chopin, Frédéric François (1810–1849)

Scherzo No. 1 in B minor

index
Op. 20
composed
1832
recorded
Emanuel Ax, 1988
report submitted
Chris Harvey, March 12, 2010

The Scherzo in B minor is divided into three main sections set in ABA form, with the A sections as scherzos and the middle section as a Trio. The Scherzo sections are furious ad rapid, and the performer brings out the violence in Chopin's music. His emotion is apparent through his playing. He demonstrates great contrast in dynamics and articulation, making the music dance as lively as possible. However, due to the extreme number of repetitions written in the piece, the performer does not provide noticeable variation across repeated sections.

Ax brings out the emotional changes via dynamics and tempo changes. He prolongs the Trio for as long as possible, creating a sense of anxiety in the listener, who longs for the expressive and delicate tonic chord that releases the built-up tension.

The Trio section is the polar opposite of the scherzo sections. Ax brings out as much, if not more, emotion to this section, but an emotion of tenderness and love rather than ferocity and angst. The sections also differ via thematic material: while the Scherzo is more motivic, the Trio contains a sweet lyrical melody in the middle voice that depicts mother and child.

The performer builds tension towards the end of the Trio, bringing out dissonances and non-chord tones. He prepares us for the return of the Scherzo with a molto ritardando and the reappearance of the introduction.

The return of the scherzo section is no different than the exposition, except that it is shorthand and with a Coda added. Ax again anticipates the Coda, building up speed. The Coda is like a warped waltz with dissonant tones and a humorous feel. The performer executes the trills, arpeggios, and scales with extreme precision.

This short report discusses the aspects of Emmanuel Ax's performance. For a detailed analysis of this very composition by Chopin, read my in-depth paper written on November 9, 2009.

Debussy, Claude (1862–1918)

Estampes, 3. Jardins Sous La Pluie

index
L 100
composed
1903
recorded
Alexis Weissenberg, 1985
report submitted
Chris Harvey, March 12, 2010

This movement is an overflow of notes and chords. Rather than focusing on individual aspects of the music, the performer aims for the overall atmosphere. Polyrhythms and other fast runs don't have to be played exactly correctly, and the bigger picture is what's important. The piece has many different sections that seem to be put together in an arbitrary manner, much like an unpredictable rainstorm. The transitions between the segments do flow smoothly, however. There is no defined form, but the melodic theme recurs throughout the movement. Most of the texture of the piece consists of simple homophonic melody and accompaniment, although the melody often switches between hands. The movement moves in and out of different modes, usually among major, minor, and augmented. There doesn't seem to be a predictable change in tonal center, but the harmonic structure is always present, even with a lot of chromaticism in a few places.

The performer exhibits great control and clarity, even at a fast pace. His choice of dynamics contradicts the text at some points, but otherwise he does a good job showing contrast between loud and soft. Weissenberg ends the piece in a huge wash of sound, making it almost impossible to interpret the small details of the music.

Gershwin, George (1898–1937)

Three Preludes for Piano

recorded
Doug Montgomery, 2008
report submitted
Chris Harvey, December 17, 2008

Gershwin's music was more similar to jazz than it was to impressionist music written in the early 20th Century. Both technically and musically challenging, Three Preludes for Piano demonstrates various styles and capabilities of the instrument and musician. Montgomery seems to hold back emotion when performing, although correctly expresses rhythm and note patterns. He hardly uses any pedal, which could be useful in some sections.
