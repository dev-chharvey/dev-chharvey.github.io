Diagrams and Descriptions

The Outer Ear

The pinna is the visible part of the outer ear. It directs sound toward the ear canal and also provides some coloration. It helps us locate the direction of a sound.

The sound is focused through the ear canal to the ear drum. The ear drum is a membrane that acts like a microphone diaphragm. It vibrates at the frequencies of the pressure waves that approach it.

The Middle Ear

The ossicles are three tiny bones (the tiniest in your body), shaped like a hammer, anvil and stirrup. They amplify the sound from your ear drum by almost 25 dB. The ossicles then transfer the sound energy to the oval window, the threshold to the Inner Ear. (The ossicles also help ease the transfer of pressure waves from gas into liquid.)

The eustachian tube is a small canal that leads from the middle ear to the oral cavity. It serves as a valve that equalizes air pressure between the middle ear and the outside world. When the valve opens (usually when swallowing), you will feel a slight popping sensation, especially when at extremely high or low altitudes. For auditory purposes, the eustachian tube helps the ossicles operate at equalized air pressure.

The Inner Ear

The oval window is a membrane between the inner ear and the middle ear. It is similar to the ear drum, passing acoustical information inward, specifically to the cochlea.

Cochlea

The cochlea is filled with a watery-like fluid called perilymph. Because of the oval window, the fluid vibrates at the frequencies passed through the ear. These frequencies move tiny hairs called cilia on the Organ of Corti. The cilia are attached to hair cells which stimulate nerve fibers. It is at this point that mechanical energy is transformed into chemical energy. The nerve endings that are stimulated will send signals to the brain, indicating the sound that we hear.

The semi-circular canals, attached to the cochlea, serve no known auditory purpose. They are three U-shaped tubes arramged in perpendicular planes, necessary for determining balance. They operate in almost the exact manner as the cochlea does. The perilymph in these canals move with respect to the force of gravity, and stimulate tiny cilia attached to nerve fibers. These Vestibular Nerve fibers send information to the brain telling us our orientation.
