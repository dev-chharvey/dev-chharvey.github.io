A comparison of different tuning systems of the modern piano

Presentation on December 7, 2009.

  • frequency and pitch
    • logarithmic relation between frequency and pitch
      • show graphs on slideshow / whiteboard
        • pitch vs. frequency
        • frequency vs. pitch
    • as pitch constantly increases, frequency will increase exponentially
      • play chromatic scale on piano
    • as frequency constantly increases, pitch will increase logarithmically
      • play soundfile
  • consonance vs. dissonance
    • harmonics
      • fundamental frequency
      • harmonics: integer multiples of fundamental
      • timbre or color: different instruments have different balances of overtones
        • the term color can more analogously be applied to pitch, however
          • frequency of light → hue (color)
          • frequency of sound → pitch
    • consonance
      • subjective term / varies by opinion / no measurable unit
      • two sounds are dissonant if they have a rough sensation to the ear
      • smaller integer ratios = more consonant
        • e.g., 2:1 is more consonant than 199:100 even though they have approximately the same value
      • corresponding multiples
        • two different fundamentals share overtones
        • example: perfect octave
          • A3: 220, 440, 660, 880, 1100, 1320, 1540, 1760, …
          • A4: 440, 880, 1320, 1760, 2200, 2640, 3080, 3520, …
        • example: perfect fifth
          • A3: 220, 440, 660, 880, 1100, 1320, 1540, 1760, 1980, …
          • E4: 330, 660, 990, 1320, 1650, 1980, 2310, 2640, 2970, …
  • Pythagorean Scale
    • Pythagoras found that P8 and P5 were consonant
    • created a scale derived from only octaves and fifths
    • each scale degree was found by adding a fifth to the previous, then subtracting the appropriate number of octaves
      • rule — 2:1 for octaves, 3:2 for fifths (taken from the first 3 harmonics)
      • multiply to increase frequency (pitch), divide to decrease
    • 'spiral of fifths'
      • If we add 12 fifths to our fundamental and subtract 7 octaves, we should end up where we started (in the modern system), but we actually end up a bit sharp. This is because our system does not use a perfect 3:2 ratio for the P5 interval, contrary to Pythagoras and the harmonic series.
      • Ditto if we subtract 12 fifths and add 7 octaves, we end up just flat of our original note.
      • the farther we deviate from the fundamental, the more out of tune we become
        • this creates a spiral of fifths because we will never end up back where we started
        • it does not overlap itself
  • Just Intonation
    • based on smaller-integer ratios for a more consonant sound
    • uses the harmonic series for deriving the scale degrees
    • do: fundamental (1st harmonic)
      so: 3rd harmonic dropped down 1 octave
      mi: 5th harmonic dropped down 2 octaves
      re: 9th harmonic dropped down 3 octaves
      ti: 15th harmonic dropped down 3 octaves
      do: 2nd harmonic
    • what happened to fa and la?
      • fa — taken from Pythagoras's system, the ratio of 4:3
        • subtract a fifth from fundamental, then add one octave
      • la — obtained by subtracting a fifth and an octave from the 5th harmonic
    • non-diatonic pitches are out of tune as they do not exist in the harmonic series, or they exist but are out of tune or too high
      • e.g., minor seventh or augmented fourth
  • Meantone Scale
    • adjustments made to the Pythagorean or Just Scales
    • uses a system of thirds based on the 5th harmonic minus 2 octaves, a ratio of 5:4
    • remaining notes interpolated as evenly as possible
      • uses square roots for midpoints, e.g., re is the midpoint of do and mi
      • fill in the spaces
    • the P5s are no longer perfect
  • Equal Temperament
    • our modern system
    • treats each key equally, as demonstrated by J.S. Bach's WTC
    • one can play more in tune in key signatures that have many sharps or flats
    • semitones are equidistant from one another in terms of ratios of frequency
      • multiply the frequency of any semitone by the twelfth-root of two (\sqrt[12]{2}) to obtain the succeeding semitone's frequency
        • suggested by Marin Mersenne (1588–1648)
      • the fundamental frequency plus 12 semitones will end up being multiplied by 2, resulting in a perfect octave (Pythagorean/harmonic octave)
      • 100 cents per semitone
        • cents are logarithmic, like pitch
        • the higher the frequency, the wider the frequency range of one cent
    • equal-tempered thirds are 14 cents sharper than perfect thirds (Pythagorean/harmonic thirds)
    • nervous and agitated mood compared to just or meantone scales
    • motivation for twelve-tone, serial, chromatic, etc. music in the 20th Century
