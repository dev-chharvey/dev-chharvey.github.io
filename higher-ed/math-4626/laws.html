Algebraic Limit Theorem for Functional Limits
Let c,k∈ℝ and ƒ1(x) and ƒ2(x) be defined ∀x∈ℝ. Then:

  1. limx→c kƒ1(x) = klimx→c ƒ1(x)
  2. limx→c (ƒ1(x)+ƒ2(x)) = limx→c ƒ1(x) + limx→c ƒ2(x)
  3. limx→c (ƒ1(x)⋅ƒ2(x)) = limx→c ƒ1(x) ⋅ limx→c ƒ2(x)
  4. limx→c (ƒ1(x)÷ƒ2(x)) = limx→c ƒ1(x) ÷ limx→c ƒ2(x), provided limx→c ƒ2(x) ≠ 0

The ALT says that the limit of a sum of functions at a point is equal to the sum of the limits of functions at that point. The same holds for multiplication and division, as long as the denominator in part (iv) is not zero. Part (i) means the limit of a scalar multiplier of a function at a point is the scalar multiplier of the limit of the function of that point.

Power Law
Let q∈ℚ+. Then:
          limx→c (ƒ1(x))q = (limx→c ƒ1(x))q

Let n1 and n2 be natural numbers, with q=n1/n2. The limit of the n1-th power or n2-th root of a function at a point is the n1-th power or the n2-th root of the limit of the function at that point.

Identity Laws

  1. limx→c k = k
  2. limx→c x = c

These laws are a consequence of the Direct Substitution method.

Direct Substitution Property
If ƒ(x) is a rational function and c is in the domain of ƒ(x), then:
limx→c ƒ(x) = ƒ(c).
