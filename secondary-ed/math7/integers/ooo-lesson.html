Lesson 1.4: Order of Operations

Preparation

Objectives

  • The student will compute the value of a numeric expression involving the four basic operations on whole numbers.
  • The student will do the same with parentheses and exponents included in the expression.

Essential Questions

  • What can multiplication and division look like?
  • How do I know which operations to perform first?
  • What kinds of grouping symbols can we use?
  • What is an exponent?

Vocabulary

term
a number that is part of a sum or difference
evaluate
to figure out the value of an expression

Lesson Implementation

Warm-up

Hand students the Math Facts worksheet. Tell them that they have 5 minutes to complete as many problems as possible. This will get the wheels turning and get them ready for the lesson. It can also serve as a game or contest.

Give students the following problems to complete and turn in. Check for understanding.

  1. 15\div 5+1\times 18
  2. (20-2)\times(8+2)+10

Instruction

First introduce notation for multiplication and division.

Different ways multiplication can appear:

  • cross: 3\times 5
  • dot: 3\cdot 5
  • juxtaposition of constants: 3(5), (3)5, or (3)(5)
  • juxtaposition of variables: 3x

Different ways division can appear:

  • division sign: 15\div 3
  • fraction: \frac{15}{3}

Students should practice turning multiplication signs into juxtaposition before performing any operations. Likewise, turn division signs into fractions. This will help students see the terms in an expression, so they need not worry about memorizing the order of operations.

Steps to perform to evaluate an expression:

  1. Turn all multiplication into juxtaposition and division into fractions.
  2. Count and simplify each term.
  3. Add or subtract the terms from left to right.

Optional Discussion

Why does multiplication come before addition?

Multiplication of whole numbers is defined as repeated addition. For example, 3\times 5 = 3+3+3+3+3 and 5\times 3 = 5+5+5. Students do not yet need to be aware that multiplication is commutative, however they will most likely derive this fact anyway.

Since multiplication can be changed into addition, we can change all multiplication problems into addition problems.

Use a multiplication problem from the warm-up as an example; give 1 to 2 practice problems.

Now give a multiplication problem such as 2+3\times 5. Ask, What should I do first? Well, we know what multiplication is: it's really addition! Then change the problem to 2+3+3+3+3+3 and ask them to compute.

Introduce the idea of grouping symbols. List the types of grouping symbols students might see.

Types of grouping symbols:

  • parentheses: \left(3+5\right)
  • brackets: \left[3+5\right]
  • fraction bar: \frac{11+5}{3+5}

How do grouping symbols work? Everything in parentheses and brackets are grouped. In a fraction, everything in the numerator is grouped, and everything in the denominator is grouped.

We must simplify the expression in the grouping symbols first, before doing anything outside the grouping symbols.

Hand students the Exponents mini-lesson. Quickly review what an exponent really is (repeated multiplication) and how to calculate it on the calculator. Have them do some example problems. This whole process should take about 15 to 20 minutes.

Exponents practice problems.

Expand the following:

  1. 3^8
  2. n^7
  3. a^2 b^4

Write each as an exponent:

  1. 2\cdot 2\cdot 2
  2. 4\cdot 4\cdot 5\cdot 5\cdot 5
  3. aaabbc

Guided Practice

Have students work on these problems independently or in groups.

Practice problems:

  1. 5+10\times 10
  2. 6\times 7 + 1
  3. 5\times 1+9+5\div 1
  4. 10\times 4+12+5-2
  5. 6+11\times 4+12\div 12
  6. 12\times 10+5\times 5+8
  7. 14\div 2\times 8+3
  8. (20-2)\times(8+2)+10
  9. (10+52-2)\div(9-4)
  10. 6+\frac{3\times 8+8}{4}

Introduce problems involving expressions where the exponent is applied to monomials only (below). After grouping symbols, they should compute the exponents, and then use the process learned from yesterday.

  1. 5+8^2+6
  2. 4+\left(9+2^2\div 2\right)
  3. 3\times\left(2\times 4^3\right)\div 4

After practicing with monomials, introduce problems with exponents applied to binomials. Stress the importance of evaluating the parentheses before computing the exponent.

  1. 8+\left(5+2\right)^2
  2. 3^2+\left(24\div 3+3^2\right)
  3. \left[\left(10-3\right)^2\times 2\right]+7^2
